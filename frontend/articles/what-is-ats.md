# What Is an ATS, Really?

If you've job hunted in the last decade, you've probably heard that "a robot reads your resume before a human does." That's mostly true, and it's worth understanding exactly what that robot is and isn't.

## It's a filing system, not a judge

An ATS — Applicant Tracking System — is software companies use to collect, organize, and search applications. Think of it less like a bouncer deciding who gets in, and more like a filing cabinet with a search bar. When a recruiter opens a role, hundreds of resumes might come in. The ATS lets them search and sort that pile instead of reading every single one cold.

Most ATS platforms do a few things:

- Parse your resume into fields (name, contact info, job titles, dates, skills)
- Let recruiters search or filter by keyword, years of experience, or location
- Track where a candidate is in the hiring process
- Sometimes, rank or score candidates against the job description

That last one — ranking and scoring — is the part that gets mythologized. It's real, but it's usually a starting point for a human to look at, not an automatic rejection machine.

## What actually trips up an ATS

The myth is that you need secret keywords to "beat" the system. The reality is simpler and less mysterious: **most ATS problems are parsing problems, not scoring problems.**

Formatting choices that commonly confuse parsers:

- Text inside tables, text boxes, or headers/footers (some parsers skip these entirely)
- Multi-column layouts read in the wrong order (left column, then right, versus what looks visually correct)
- Unusual fonts or heavy graphics replacing plain text
- Dates or job titles in inconsistent formats

If a parser can't correctly extract your job titles and dates, it doesn't matter how qualified you are — that information effectively doesn't exist to the system. This is why WrenPath includes a single-column ATS mode: not because two columns are wrong, but because some parsers still read them incorrectly, and a resume that a human finds a little less visually interesting is better than one a machine can't read at all.

## What it doesn't do

An ATS doesn't understand nuance. It doesn't know that "cash management" on a bank teller's resume means something different from "cash management" on a corporate treasury analyst's resume — it just sees a match. It doesn't know if you're exaggerating or underselling. It doesn't care about your career story.

That's exactly the gap WrenPath is built to fill. Getting past the parser is table stakes. What actually gets you hired is a resume that's both machine-readable *and* honestly represents what you've done — which is a human problem, not a formatting problem.

## The real takeaway

Don't build your resume around outsmarting a robot. Build it to be clearly formatted, honestly written, and specific about what you actually did. That happens to be exactly what gets through an ATS cleanly *and* what makes a recruiter want to call you.
