# Honest Self-Marketing: Talking About What You've Actually Done

There are two common ways people get their resume wrong, and they're opposites. One is inflating — claiming skills or scope you don't really have, hoping no one checks. The other is underselling — doing real, valuable work and describing it so flatly that it disappears on the page. WrenPath exists mostly to fix the second problem, but it's worth understanding both.

## Inflating doesn't actually work

Exaggerating gets you an interview you can't back up, or a job you're not equipped for on day one — neither is a win. Beyond that, it's just not necessary as often as people think. Most people have more real, specific accomplishments sitting in their work history than they give themselves credit for. The fix usually isn't inventing more — it's noticing what's already there.

## Underselling is the more common problem

This is the one worth focusing on, because it's sneaky. It doesn't feel like a mistake — it feels like modesty, or just "how you talk about work." A few patterns to watch for in your own resume:

- **Describing duties instead of impact.** "Responsible for customer service" describes a job description. "Resolved escalated complaints in a way that kept repeat customers, not just closed tickets" describes what you actually did and why it mattered.
- **Leaving out things because they felt like "just part of the job."** Training a new hire, covering for your manager, fixing a recurring process problem — these often don't make the resume because they didn't feel like a separate accomplishment at the time. They are.
- **Assuming a skill doesn't count if you weren't formally titled for it.** You don't need "Project Manager" in your title to have run a project. If you planned it, coordinated the people involved, and saw it through, that's real project management experience, whatever your title said.

## How to catch this in your own resume

The reflective questions WrenPath asks are built around a simple idea: most job titles come with a cluster of responsibilities that are common but not universal, and people often did more of that cluster than they wrote down. Being asked directly — "did you do X?" — surfaces things that "just describe your job" doesn't.

You can do a version of this yourself without any tool: for each bullet point on your resume, ask "would a coworker who watched me do this every day agree this is an accurate, complete description?" Often the honest answer is "accurate, but incomplete" — and that gap is where real experience is hiding.

## The line to hold

The goal isn't to sound impressive. It's to describe, specifically and honestly, what you actually did and what happened as a result. That standard rules out both exaggeration and false modesty — and it happens to be exactly what a hiring manager is trying to find underneath every resume they read.
