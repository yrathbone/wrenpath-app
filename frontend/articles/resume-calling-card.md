# Your Resume Is a Calling Card — Keep It Ready

Most people treat their resume like a fire extinguisher: forgotten in a closet until there's an emergency, then grabbed in a panic and hoped it still works.

That approach almost always shows. A resume written in a rush, reconstructed from memory under deadline pressure, misses things — the project you led eight months ago, the process you improved, the software you picked up along the way. Not because you didn't do them, but because you weren't keeping track when they happened.

## Think of it as a living document, not an event

A calling card is something you keep current because you never know when you'll need to hand it over. Your resume works the same way. The opportunities that matter most — a former colleague mentioning an opening, a recruiter reaching out, a role you didn't expect to want until you saw it — tend to arrive without warning. If your resume is ready, you can act on them immediately. If it isn't, you're stuck writing it from scratch while the window closes.

## What "keeping it ready" actually looks like

You don't need to rewrite your resume every month. A few small habits do most of the work:

- **When something noteworthy happens at work, write it down somewhere — anywhere.** A note in your phone, a running document, whatever you'll actually use. Capture it the week it happens, while the specifics (the number, the outcome, the tool you used) are still fresh.
- **Revisit it a few times a year, not just when you're job hunting.** A quick pass every quarter to add anything new takes ten minutes. Reconstructing a year of work from memory takes hours and loses detail.
- **Update it after a role change, a big project, or a new skill** — not after you've already started applying somewhere.

## Why this connects to being honest on paper

There's a second, quieter benefit to keeping a resume current: it keeps you honest with yourself about what you're actually doing at work. When you sit down periodically and ask "what did I do that's worth noting," you start noticing your own growth in real time instead of trying to reconstruct it later — which is exactly the kind of honest self-assessment WrenPath's reflective questions are designed to prompt. The habit of noticing is the same skill either way.

## The takeaway

A resume that's always ready isn't about being perpetually job-searching. It's about not being caught flat-footed when an opportunity you didn't plan for shows up — and about actually remembering everything you've accomplished, instead of underselling yourself because the details faded.
